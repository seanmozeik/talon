# Plating Composition

Plating is not decoration. It's the final transmission of the dish's intent. A plate tells the diner: "This was considered. This was intentional. You're not eating survival food; you're eating thought."

The framework: composition (where things sit), color (visual interest), height (dimension), sauce (the glue), and negative space (what's *not* there).

## Composition

Start with the protein or primary element. It's rarely centered (too symmetrical reads as static). Off-center, resting at roughly 4–5 o'clock on a round plate. From there:

- **Secondary vegetable** — supports the protein. If protein is lean, give it a sauce or rich vegetable underneath. If protein is rich, give it bright vegetable (fermented, raw, acidic).
- **Starch or base** — if present, anchors the plate. Grains, puree, or bread. Not always needed.
- **Garnish and accent** — herbs, fermented elements, crispy bits. These add texture and flavor pop.
- **Sauce** — not pooled underneath the protein (traps steam, softens bottom). Sauce pools behind or beside, creating a gloss that reads as "finished."

The rule: protein first (position), vegetable second (support), sauce third (gloss), garnish last (pop).

## Color and Visual Rhythm

Monochrome plates (all green, all white) feel boring. A plate needs:
- At least two colors (protein + vegetable + sauce usually handles this)
- One bright accent (herb green, red ferment, golden-fried garnish)
- Contrast between components (dark protein, pale vegetable, bright sauce)

Look at a plated dish and answer: "Can I identify three distinct items?" If not, it reads as sludge.

Example: [[Dish - Charred Spring Onion]] = charred-black onion (protein), white whipped curd (vegetable), green herb oil (sauce), fermented onion brine (accent gloss). Three distinct colors, clear visual hierarchy.

> [!note]
> Color isn't artifice. It's information. Red ferment tells the diner "this is tangy and aged." Green oil says "fresh, bright." Dark char says "seared, intentional." Use color to communicate flavor intent.

## Height and Dimension

A flat plate feels flat. Add height:
- Stack or lean the protein
- Pile garnish on top (microgreens, crispy skin, herb fronds)
- Use sauce height (foam, quenelle, drip) to create visual line

Height doesn't mean precarious. A garnish that blows off the plate when a diner shifts their chair is annoying, not impressive. Stability is part of technique.

## Negative Space

The plate edge is important. If every millimeter is covered, the plate feels cramped. Leave 1–2 inches at the top (diner's right hand, where they won't accidentally touch) and 0.5–1 inch of breathing room between components. This creates visual calm and makes the food look intentional, not crammed.

A crowded plate is ambitious; a composed plate is confident.

## Sauce Philosophy

Sauce on the plate serves two purposes: gloss (it makes the plate look finished) and flavor (it supports the bite). A small sauce pool behind the protein is enough. A generous smear (not a pool) under and behind also works. An entire plate drowned in sauce overwhelms the plate and makes it slippery.

[[Sauce Mothers]] should taste like an edit of the plate's intent, not a separate element. If the plate is about charred onion and whipped curd, the sauce should echo those elements (fermented onion juice + curd brine) rather than a different flavor entirely.

## Temperature Zones

A plate can have zones of different temperatures:
- Hot: protein, warm sauce
- Room temp: vegetable, base
- Cold: garnish (if appropriate)

This complexity is advanced. For most plates, hot protein + room-temp vegetable + warm sauce is the frame.

> [!warning]
> A plate plated too far ahead (more than 3 mins) cools down, sauces set, and the plate reads as "made earlier," not "just finished." Time your plating to fire. In service, plating is the last step before the plate goes to the table.

## Plating Discipline

Every cook plates the same dish the same way. Consistency means a diner eating from two different servers sees the same plate composition. This is not creativity; it's brand consistency. Creativity happens in the menu; execution happens at plating.

Use mise position (see [[Mise en Place Philosophy]]) to enable this: sauce always lives in the same squeeze bottle, positioned at 5 o'clock. Garnish lives in the same container. This way, plating is a sequence, not a decision.
