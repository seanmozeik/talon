# Stock Theory

Stock is bones + water + time + heat. Collagen in bone dissolves as gelatin; proteins and minerals leach out; aromatics infuse. The result: a foundational liquid rich in umami (from amino acids and nucleotides), body (from gelatin), and flavor (from aromatics). It's the baseline that nearly every savory sauce, soup, and braise rests on.

The difference between great stock and bad stock is ratio, time, and filtration. The difference between good stock and exceptional stock is the question of what bones you use and whether you care about clarity.

## Types and Their Purpose

**Light poultry stock** — chicken bones (wings, carcasses, feet). Simmer 4–6 hours. Flavor: neutral, delicate, clean. Body: light. Use: poultry dishes, light sauces, soups where flavor shouldn't dominate. Cost: cheap (scrap bones).

**Fish stock** — white fish bones (cod, halibut, sole; not oily fish like salmon). No heads unless gutted (gills = bitter). Simmer 30–45 minutes *only*—longer extracts bitterness. Flavor: marine, mineral. Body: light. Use: fish sauces, seafood reductions, clam broths. Cost: moderate (good bones aren't free).

**Dark veal or beef stock** — bones roasted first (200°C, 30 mins) then simmered 8–12 hours. Flavor: deep, rich, almost meaty. Body: heavy (high collagen yield). Use: red meat sauces, braises, demi-glace. Cost: high (long cooking + fuel).

**Vegetable stock** — aromatics (onion, carrot, celery), herbs (thyme, bay, parsley), sometimes leftover vegetable scraps. Simmer 30 mins (longer = muddy). Flavor: depends on ratio; use as neutral base only if not over-boiled. Body: minimal. Cost: very cheap. Use: vegetable soups, cooking grains, stretching meat stocks.

## The Method

1. **Clean bones** (optional) — blanching removes scum quickly; most cooks skip this (time cost).
2. **Roast (if dark stock)** — bones in 200°C oven until caramelized, 20–30 mins. This adds [[Maillard Reaction]] depth.
3. **Combine with aromatics** — onion (halved, unpeeled for color), carrot, celery, water. Proportion: roughly 2 lbs bones per gallon of water.
4. **Bring to simmer, never boil** — boiling emulsifies impurities; simmering lets them fall to the bottom. Skim any foam first 15 minutes, then leave alone.
5. **Add herbs/spices last** — after 2 hours of cooking. Thyme, bay leaf, peppercorns (not salt—season at finish).
6. **Simmer low** — bubbles should break surface slowly, not rolling boil. 4–12 hours depending on bone type.
7. **Strain through cheesecloth** — catch solids, keep liquid. Pass through cloth-lined strainer for clarity if you care.
8. **Cool, skim fat** — stock thickens as it cools (gelatin sets). Skim fat layer for clarity or keep it (adds mouth-feel).

A well-made stock should gel when cold (sign of collagen extracted). If it stays liquid, either bones were low-collagen (chicken feet yield more) or time was short.

## Time vs. Quality

Time matters, but with limits:

- **Poultry**: 4–6 hours yields good extraction; 8 hours adds diminishing returns
- **Fish**: 30–45 mins; longer extracts bitterness (faster extraction on small bones)
- **Beef/veal**: 8+ hours is justified; at 12 hours you're stable; beyond 16 hours (next day), no gain
- **Vegetable**: 30 mins, max. Longer = mushy flavor

The first few hours extract most flavor and amino acids. Cooking longer extracts minerals and collagen but refines rather than transforms. Don't obsess over 14 hours vs. 12; aim for "good baseline" not "absolute maximum."

## Salting

Don't salt stock. Salt added now is locked in; if stock reduces later, salt concentrates. Always salt at finish (sauce, soup, braise). This is discipline—stock is neutral raw material, not finished product.

## Fermented Stock

We've experimented with adding fermented vegetable scraps (bits from [[Lacto-Fermentation]] that don't make the cut) to stock in the last 30 minutes. The lactic acid from fermentation adds subtle tang and umami. After straining, the stock reads as "deeper" without tasting overtly fermented. This is good technique: use scraps, gain flavor.

## Yields and Storage

A pot of stock (roughly 2 gallons) reduces down to 1.5–1.75 gallons depending on simmer intensity and aromatics evaporation. Yield is high; one roast session makes a week's worth of sauce base.

**Storage:**
- Room temperature: 1 day (if kept above 60°C) or cool quickly and refrigerate
- Refrigerated: 4–5 days (lactobacilli will colonize if left longer)
- Frozen: 3–6 months (flavor stable; body intact when thawed)

Freeze in ice cube trays for portion control, or 1-liter containers for batch use.

> [!warning]
> Stock left above 40°C for more than 4 hours is a bacterial risk. Cool quickly (ice bath, shock into walk-in), or don't make it. Hot-holding at 60°C+ is safe only during service.

## Clarity

Clear stock is beautiful but labor-intensive (raft clarification, multiple strainings). We clarify for consommé or special reductions; otherwise, clean straining (cheesecloth, settled overnight) is enough. A slightly cloudy stock tastes just as good; clarity is aesthetics, not flavor.
