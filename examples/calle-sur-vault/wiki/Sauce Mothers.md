# Sauce Mothers

A sauce mother is a foundational liquid: stock, reduction, emulsion, or fermented base that becomes the backbone of service sauces. The French canon names five (béchamel, velouté, espagnole, hollandaise, tomato). We use that structure loosely—what matters is building from a neutral base outward.

The principle: taste in layers. Start with clean, deep base (stock). Adjust acid and seasoning. Finish with texture (liaison, butter, foam, vegetables) and fresh flavor (herbs, juice, fermented condiment).

## Stock as Foundation

[[Stock Theory]] is where this starts. A neutral, collagen-rich stock (bones, aromatics, long simmer) becomes the canvas. We make three stocks on rotation:

- **Light poultry** — chicken bones, neutral aromatics
- **Fish** — bones (not heads, which bitter), white wine, short simmer
- **Dark** — beef or veal bones, roasted, long simmer, depth

Each stock tastes different and suits different dishes.

## The Build

1. **Base stock** — clean, neutral, balanced (not over-salted; salt at finish)
2. **Reduction** — simmer to concentrate. Time = depth. Reducing by half intensifies flavor and develops body via collagen -> gelatin
3. **Acid adjustment** — lemon, vinegar, fermented vegetable juice, wine. This brightens and balances richness. Taste and adjust.
4. **Liaison or thickening** — beurre manié (butter+flour), egg yolk, starch slurry, or just reduction (all reduce, thicken texture)
5. **Finish** — fresh herbs, fermented condiment (koji shio, miso, aged ferment), or cold butter (monter au beurre)

This isn't formula; it's structure. Sauce is fundamentally about taking one flavor (stock) and refining it into three (base, acid, finish).

## Fermented Finishes

We've been finishing reduction-based sauces with aged vegetable ferment juice (from [[Lacto-Fermentation]] batches). A few tablespoons of bright, tangy ferment liquid added at the very end adds acid *and* umami—lactobacilli produce both. The sauce tastes clean and alive instead of flat.

Example: roasted lamb sauce = dark stock + red wine reduction + fermented spring onion juice + finish with cold butter. The ferment adds a subtle, funky brightness that reads as "professional."

> [!tip]
> Don't add ferment early; heat kills the bacteria and volatilizes some aroma. Add at the very end, or splash into the plated sauce so the flavors stay distinct.

## Pan Sauces (Ad Hoc)

After searing, fond (browned bits) stays in the pan. Deglaze with wine or stock, scrape, reduce by half, finish with cold butter and herbs. 60 seconds, and you have a sauce rich with [[Maillard Reaction]] depth. This is sauce-making distilled: build flavor, not ingredients.

## Emulsions

Hollandaise (butter + egg yolk + acid) and aioli (emulsion of oil + garlic + egg yolk) are mothers too—they can be flavored and refined endlessly. Warm emulsions are delicate (temperature and whisking matter); cold emulsions are stable. Both require balance of acid and fat.

We make herb hollandaise (fresh herbs blended in at finish), fermented garlic aioli (jarred fermented garlic in place of fresh), and red pepper aioli (roasted red pepper, lemon, egg, oil).

## Costing Considerations

A mother sauce uses cheap cuts (bones), time (labor), and stock (energy cost is real). The yield is high (a pot of dark sauce serves 60 covers or more). But the *visible cost* is labor. Runners see sauce as "just sauce," not as built asset.

Document the cost of a batch (bones + scraps + aromatics + 4 hours of simmering). Even if they're re-costs at $0.50/oz, that's real margin. Sauces are where technique becomes capital.

> [!note]
> Mother sauces sound formal. They're not. A good sauce is: stock + reduction + acid + finish. Everything else is refinement.

## Service Discipline

Keep mothers in labeled containers, dated. Taste before service—fermented batches can develop unexpected funk if temperature-abused. A sauce that smells off goes out; service discipline saves reputation.
