# Costing Fundamentals

Costing is the discipline of knowing exactly what each plate costs in ingredients and variable labor, so that you can price menus with intention and understand margins.

Most restaurants don't cost carefully. They guess ("30% food cost is standard"), build a menu, then discover they're broke. Worse, they don't know which dishes make money and which lose it.

We cost every dish. Every ingredient is priced (wholesale cost, supplier, unit). Every labor component is assigned (prep time, cooking time, plating time). A plate costs X; you price it at 2.5–3X gross margin (depending on service style and market).

## Ingredient Costing

**By-the-pound cost** — A kilo of fermented spring onions costs Maria (material) $8. We buy in bulk; the effective cost is $8/kg or $0.50/oz. Plated at 4 oz, that's $2 per cover's vegetable component.

**Scaling to cover** — fermented vegetables, stocks, sauces are batch-produced. A pot of dark stock (see [[Stock Theory]]) costs roughly $12 (bones + aromatics + fuel) and yields 1.5 gallons or roughly 180 oz. At $0.07/oz, a 4 oz sauce portion costs $0.28.

**Waste factor** — raw vegetables have trim. A whole radish (4 oz) trims to 3 oz after cleaning. Trim rate = 25%. Costing accounts for this: you price radish by *final usable weight*, not as-purchased. Some suppliers quote "trim already accounted for" (pre-cleaned); others don't.

**Fermented vegetable batch cost** — a week's fermentation of spring onions: 5 lbs onions ($3 wholesale), 2.5 oz salt ($0.10), container + time ($1 overhead). Total: $4.10 for raw batch, yielding roughly 4.5 lbs fermented (water loss). Cost per oz: $0.09. Plated at 4 oz: $0.36 ingredient cost.

## Labor Costing

Ingredient cost is only part of plate cost. Labor is real.

**Prep time** — fermentation batches take (time labor) 15 mins to assemble, 2 weeks to age (passive), 10 mins to portion and label. Labor is roughly $0.25 per batch's portions (if you value prep labor at $25/hour, overhead allocated across batch).

**Cooking time** — a seared protein (30 seconds fire) is negligible labor. A roasted vegetable (prep + roast + plating) is 5 mins hands-on. At $25/hour, that's $2 labor cost.

**Plating time** — a simple plate (protein + vegetable + sauce, 90 seconds) is $0.60 labor. A complex plate (multiple elements, micro-garnish, 3 mins) is $1.25 labor.

Most restaurants ignore plating labor. It's a mistake. Plating is direct labor; it should be costed.

## Plate Costing Example

**Charred Spring Onion plate:**
- Fermented spring onion (4 oz): $0.36
- Whipped curd (3 oz): $0.45 (dairy is expensive; cost varies)
- Herb oil (0.5 oz): $0.20
- Fermentation brine (1 oz): $0.01
- Garnish and accent: $0.30
- Stock/other: $0.10
- **Ingredient subtotal: $1.42**
- Prep labor (allocated): $0.15
- Plating labor (allocated): $0.60
- **Total plate cost: $2.17**

At a selling price of $6 (per cover as a course), gross margin is $3.83 (64%). Net margin (after front-of-house, utilities, rent) is roughly 15–25% depending on overhead.

> [!warning]
> Costing is only useful if prices are accurate and updated. A supplier increases price 20%; you don't update costing; your margin evaporates. Costing should be revisited monthly, especially for high-volume ingredients.

## Seasonal Cost Variance

[[Seasonality Calendar]] drives cost variance. A dish featuring asparagus in May (moderate cost) might cost 40% more in July when asparagus is imported. Rather than absorb the cost (margin drops), restaurants change the menu. The framework stays; the vegetable shifts. This is why seasonality and costing are linked.

## Contribution Margin

A dish might be 60% gross margin (ingredient + direct labor) but have low net margin if it's labor-intensive or uses expensive components. Contribution margin is what's left after direct costs and what funds overhead (rent, utilities, insurance, management labor).

A 40% gross margin dish might have 20% net margin after accounting for everything. Price accordingly.

## Transparency and Staff

Cooks often don't know the cost of ingredients. This is a mistake. A cook who knows that duck fat costs $0.50/oz respects it more than a cook who doesn't. Transparency builds discipline. We post ingredient costs in the walk-in and update them monthly.
