# Seasonality Calendar

Seasonality isn't nostalgia or cliché. It's the foundation of how we cost menus, plan [[Fermented Hot Sauce Line|ferments]], and decide what's on the pass each week. A vegetable in season is cheap, flavorful, and abundant; out of season it's expensive and flavorless. The two are not the same dish.

We work with [[Salt Marsh Farm]] (Maria) and [[Costera Fish]] (Renato). Their rhythms are our rhythms.

## Spring (March–May)

**Vegetables** — alliums (spring onions, young garlic), brassicas (young cabbage, radishes), tender lettuces, early peas, asparagus (late April+), artichokes.

**Fish** — white fish (halibut, cod, sole coming in), small crustaceans (spring shrimp, scallops), young sardines.

**Ferments** — spring vegetables ferment quickly (16–22°C temps, tender vegetables). We move fast: onions and radishes are fermented by week 2–3. Bright, sharp ferments.

**Cost** — moderate. Spring is transition (not peak abundance, but supply exceeds winter). Margins are okay.

**Menu implications** — we favor vegetables as primary (see [[Vegetable-Forward Cooking]]). [[Spring 2026 Menu]] leans into fermented onion, fava beans (late spring), tender greens. No heavy stocks; focus is on fresh, acidic, alive plates.

## Early Summer (June–early July)

**Vegetables** — stone fruit precursors, early zucchini and summer squash, green beans, corn (late June), tomatoes (late June, still pricey).

**Fish** — peak abundance. Smaller fish (mackerel, sardines, sea bass), good protein yields. Prices drop 20–30% vs. spring.

**Ferments** — hot ferments accelerate (28–32°C). We switch to warm-ferment hot sauce base (see [[Fermented Hot Sauce Line]]). Ferments taste punchy and evolved by week 2.

**Cost** — low. Abundance = cheap. Gross margins jump.

**Menu implications** — heavier proteins enter. Fish stocks become viable (not stock-based, but fish in stock as feature). Vegetable sides are abundant; cost of goods allows richer preparation.

## Mid–Late Summer (July–August)

**Vegetables** — tomatoes, eggplant, zucchini, peppers (late July+), peaches, stone fruit. Fermented vegetable inventory is high (backlog of spring/early-summer ferments).

**Fish** — abundance continues, but some species start rotating out. Smaller fish still cheap; larger proteins (halibut, snapper) start climbing.

**Ferments** — we have inventory. New ferments are started and taste aggressive (warm ferments develop fast). Risk of over-fermentation; close monitoring.

**Cost** — stable low for vegetables, moderate for proteins.

**Menu implications** — menu composition shifts to grains and heavier vegetables (eggplant, peppers). Stock-forward dishes enter (slow braises, thick soups). We're running [[Fermented Hot Sauce Line]] production (bottling, labeling) in August for fall sales.

## Fall (September–October)

**Vegetables** — transition back. Root vegetables (carrots, parsnips, beets) appear, brassicas return, mushrooms start, apples, pears.

**Fish** — some species return (mackerel, smaller bass). Larger proteins climb; white fish becomes seasonal and pricey.

**Ferments** — cool ferments restart (16–20°C temps favor slow, subtle ferments). We start batches that'll peak in November–December.

**Cost** — vegetables moderate, proteins rising.

**Menu implications** — stocks and braises return. [[Stock Theory]]-heavy menus. Root vegetables get [[Maillard Reaction|roasted]] and featured. Fermented root vegetables (carrot, radish, beet) are starting (won't peak until month 2).

## Winter (November–February)

**Vegetables** — stored roots (carrots, parsnips, beets, squash), hardy greens (kale, chard, chicory), citrus (late Nov+), preserved (ferments, pickles, jams from earlier in year).

**Fish** — some white fish returns (halibut, cod). Smaller fish is scarce; prices high. Shellfish (mussels, clams) are in season (better flavor profile in cold months).

**Ferments** — cold ferments have been aging since fall. We're harvesting month-old batches (settled, subtle, integrated). New ferments are started (won't mature until spring).

**Cost** — vegetables cheap (abundance of stored roots, low freshness premium). Proteins expensive (smaller supply).

**Menu implications** — [[Stock Theory]] and braises dominate. Vegetables are hearty, not delicate. We feature fermented vegetables (they've aged well). No fresh berry garnish; focus is on preserved and dry elements.

## Cost and Planning

Seasonality drives [[Costing Fundamentals]]. A dish featuring asparagus in May (moderate cost) becomes a loss leader in July (asparagus is flown in, expensive). Rather than chase out-of-season ingredients, we change the menu. The framework stays (protein + vegetable + sauce), but the vegetable shifts with season.

This discipline—accepting seasonality rather than fighting it—is how restaurants survive on thin margins.

## [[Salt Marsh Spring 2026]] and Current Cycle

Maria's farm is now in late spring (late April). Crops: young cabbage (fermenting), spring onions (fermenting), early peas, young garlic, tender lettuces. By June 1, ferments will have aged 4–6 weeks; we'll start harvesting for menu use. By July, ferment inventory is deep and we're transitioning to new crops.

> [!tip]
> Seasonality is a tool, not a constraint. It's the difference between "we can't get tomatoes" (excuse) and "tomatoes aren't in season, so we feature root vegetables instead" (intention).
