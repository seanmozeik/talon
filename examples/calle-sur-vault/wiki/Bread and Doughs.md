# Bread and Doughs

Bread is fermented grain. The fermentation (sourdough levain or commercial yeast + time) serves two purposes: develops flavor and creates structure (gluten network + gas). A dough without fermentation is dense and bland. A dough with proper fermentation is open, tangy, and alive.

We don't run a bread program at Calle Sur (closed in late 2024), but understanding dough fermentation is essential for [[Mise en Place Philosophy|prep]] and for understanding [[Fermentation Fundamentals|fermentation]] broadly.

## Levain and Wild Fermentation

**Levain** — a dough culture of flour + water + wild lactobacilli and yeasts. Fed regularly (discard half, feed with fresh flour and water), it develops flavor and becomes the leavening for sourdough bread.

- **Active levain** — bubbly, fed daily, ready to use for bread (8–12 hours old)
- **Maintenance levain** — fed weekly, stored cold, revived with 3–4 feedings before use
- **Mature** — several months old, stable flavor, strong fermentation power

A levain is essentially a [[Fermentation Fundamentals|fermentation]] culture. The microbes (lactobacilli + wild yeast, primarily Saccharomyces cerevisiae) consume sugars, produce acid and gas, and flavor the final dough. This is active fermentation, not just preservation.

## Dough Fermentation Timeline

**Mix** — flour, water, salt, levain (or commercial yeast). Gluten begins developing. Fermentation begins immediately.

**Bulk fermentation** — 4–8 hours at room temperature. Dough rises (gas production), develops flavor (acid from lactobacilli, esters from yeast), and strengthens structure (gluten network reorganizes). Stretch and fold every 30 mins for first 2 hours to build strength.

**Shaping** — divide dough, pre-shape, rest 20 mins, final shape. Surface tension is built; gas is retained.

**Final proof** — 2–4 hours at room temp, or overnight cold (12–16 hours at 4°C). Cold fermentation develops more flavor (slow fermentation = more flavor compounds). Room-temp proof is faster but less flavorful.

**Bake** — high heat (220°C+) to maximize oven spring (final gas expansion before crust sets). Crust develops from [[Maillard Reaction]] (browning of sugars and proteins).

## Flavor Development

Bread flavor comes entirely from fermentation. A quick-fermented dough (6 hours total) is mild and slightly sour. A long-fermented dough (24+ hours cold ferment) is complex, tangy, and funky. This is lactobacilli and yeast doing work.

A dough without fermentation (mix and bake in 2 hours) has minimal flavor, poor digestibility, and weak crumb structure.

## Gluten and Structure

Gluten is a protein network. Mixing develops it. Fermentation (time + enzymatic action from yeast + stretching) strengthens it. A well-developed dough feels silky and stretches without tearing. An under-developed dough tears and doesn't hold gas well.

Stretch and fold (or coil folds) every 30 mins during bulk fermentation builds strength without exhausting the dough. By hour 2, most dough is strong enough to hold final shape.

## Salt and Fermentation Rate

Salt slows fermentation (osmotic stress on microbes). A dough with 2% salt ferments slower than one with 1%. This is useful: more time = more flavor. Most bread uses 1.5–2% salt (about 10 g per 500 g flour).

## Temperature and Timing

**Warm** (22–24°C) — fermentation accelerates. A 4-hour bulk ferment is possible. Flavor develops but is subtle.

**Cool** (16–18°C) — fermentation slows. A 6–8 hour bulk ferment is normal. Flavor deepens.

**Cold** (4°C) — fermentation nearly stops. A 12–16 hour cold ferment develops complex flavor without over-proofing. Ideal for flavor.

The room-temp bulk ferment can be "warm bulk + cold final proof" (fast + flavorful) or "cool bulk + room-temp proof" (slow throughout, most flavorful).

> [!note]
> The sourdough trend pushed fermented bread into home kitchens. That's good. But fermentation time and temperature matter more than "sourdough" as a label. A quick levain bread (12 hours total) tastes mild. A long levain bread (24+ hours cold) tastes complex. Same levain, different timing = different bread.

## Dough Hydration and Crumb

Water percentage affects crumb structure:
- **60% hydration** — stiff dough, dense crumb, easy to handle
- **75% hydration** — standard, open crumb, requires technique
- **85%+ hydration** — very wet, open airy crumb, requires strength building

Higher hydration = more extensible dough (easier to stretch) but requires stronger gluten development to hold gas. A weak dough at high hydration spreads and doesn't rise well.

## Whole Grain and Fermentation

Whole grain flour ferments differently than white flour. Bran (in whole grain) absorbs more water and interferes with gluten. Fermentation of whole grain is slower and benefits from longer rest. Whole grain is also nutritionally dense; fermentation (especially long fermentation) breaks down phytic acid, improving mineral absorption.
