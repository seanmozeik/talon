# Salt Acid Fat Heat

Samin Nosrat's framework: salt, acid, fat, heat are the four pillars of cooking balance. Every dish tastes like something when these four are in proportion. Without balance, food tastes flat, greasy, or one-dimensional. Mastering this framework is the quickest path from competent to intentional cooking.

## Salt

Salt suppresses bitter, enhances sweet, and amplifies savory. It's also a preservative and osmotic agent (draws water out of vegetables, enabling fermentation).

**Seasoning salt** — added gradually during cooking. A dish undersalted tastes dull; oversalted tastes aggressive. Neither is recoverable by adding stock or acid. Taste constantly; salt as you go.

**Finishing salt** — added at the very end. Creates a bright, finishing pop. A pinch of sea salt on a warm dish reads as "intentional" to diners; distributed salt reads as "baseline seasoning."

**Preservation salt** — 2–3% by weight for fermentation, 20% for curing. At these concentrations, salt kills pathogens and selects for salt-tolerant bacteria. See [[Lacto-Fermentation]] and [[Fermentation Fundamentals]].

**Salt in fermentation** — salt doesn't just preserve; it integrates into flavor matrices over time. An aged ferment tastes "rounder" than a fresh vegetable + salt; the salt has bonded with compounds, softening sharpness.

## Acid

Acid brightens, balances richness, and activates other flavors. A dish without acid tastes muted and heavy.

**Lemon juice** — bright, clean, short shelf life in a finished sauce (flavor fades). Add to individual portions or at fire for maximum impact.

**Vinegar** — more stable than lemon; longer acidic shelf life. Red wine vinegar, sherry vinegar, aged balsamic add layered flavor (not just sour).

**Fermented vegetable juice** — from [[Lacto-Fermentation]]. Adds acid *and* umami and subtle funk. A few tablespoons in a reduction reads as "deep" to diners who can't identify the ingredient. We use this in [[Sauce Mothers]] finishes.

**Wine** — acidity + tannins + fruit. Red wine in dark sauces, white wine in lighter sauces. Reducing wine concentrates acid and evaporates alcohol.

**Buttermilk, yogurt, sour cream** — acidic dairy. Use in marinades (tenderizes), dressings (body + acid), or finishing creams.

When balancing richness (fat, stock, cream), acid is the counterpoint. A rich sauce tastes greasy without acid; acid tastes thin without fat. This is the dance.

## Fat

Fat carries flavor, creates mouthfeel, and enables browning (see [[Maillard Reaction]]). Fat without salt or acid tastes greasy. Fat with balance tastes luxurious.

**Butter** — 80% fat, emulsified with milk solids. Monter au beurre (finishing with cold butter) makes a sauce silky. Burn point is low (170°C); don't use for searing.

**Oil** — 100% fat. Higher burn point (200–240°C depending on type). Use for searing, frying, dressing. Olive oil for finishing (fresh flavor); neutral oil for cooking.

**Animal fat** — rendered pork (manteca), duck, beef. High flavor; historical prestige. We use duck fat for searing proteins, pork fat in pastry. Cost is real but flavor return is justifiable.

**Cream** — fat + milk solids + water. Lighter than butter; tempers acidity. Finish rich sauces with a small splash (1–2 oz per cup of sauce) rather than heavy cream at the start. Cream without acid tastes cloying.

Fat melts into other molecules, creating texture and gloss. A plate without any fat reads as "skinny." A plate with balanced fat reads as "finished."

## Heat

Heat controls texture, speed, and flavor development. Low heat = gentle extraction, tender results. High heat = browning, speed, risk of burning.

**Gentle heat** (60–90°C) — poaching, gentle simmering. Proteins stay tender; collagen dissolves slowly. Used for delicate items (fish, eggs, custard).

**Medium heat** (90–140°C) — regular simmering. Stock extraction, slow braises. No browning; collagen converts to gelatin over hours.

**Medium-high heat** (140–180°C) — browning without heavy char. Roasting vegetables, searing proteins. [[Maillard Reaction]] begins around 140°C and intensifies toward 180°C.

**High heat** (180°C+) — searing, frying, final crust. Quick browning; risk of burn if you're not attentive.

Heat and time trade off. Short time + high heat = seared exterior, rare interior. Long time + low heat = tender interior, no crust. Choose the profile you want.

> [!tip]
> Taste constantly at every stage (salt), adjust acid last (so you can taste it fresh), add fat at finish or during (so it melts and integrates), and use heat intentionally (never apologetically).

## The Balance

A balanced dish has:
- **Salt**: present, not aggressive
- **Acid**: bright, not vinegary
- **Fat**: silky, not greasy
- **Heat texture**: intentional (seared, tender, crispy, whatever you chose)

If something tastes flat, add salt or acid (most common fix). If something tastes acidic/light, add fat or salt. If something tastes greasy, add acid. If texture is wrong, heat is the variable (sear longer, simmer lower, etc.).

This framework applies to [[Vegetable-Forward Cooking]], [[Plating Composition]], sauces, soups, braises, everything. It's not dogma; it's a thinking tool.
