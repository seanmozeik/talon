# Knife Skills Canon

Sharp knives, consistent cuts, and discipline. Knife skills aren't fancy; they're foundational. A dull knife is dangerous (slips more) and slow. An inconsistent cut means uneven cooking. Discipline means the same cut every time, which trains speed and allows consistency to feel automatic.

We run knife drills every Tuesday morning in the walk-in, rotating through shapes (julienne, brunoise, bâtonnet, chiffonade). New hires (Jonas started as dishwasher; he did these drills) emerge capable after 4 weeks.

## Sharpness

A sharp knife should shave arm hair without pressure. If it doesn't, it needs honing (realigning the edge) or sharpening (removing steel to form a new edge).

**Honing** — weekly, before service. A steel (or ceramic rod) realigns the edge's microscopic teeth. 5 passes per side, light pressure. Doesn't remove steel; maintains edge.

**Sharpening** — monthly or when honing no longer works. Whetstones (1000–6000 grit progression) or professional sharpening. A professional sharpen costs $1–2 per knife; DIY on a whetstone costs time but builds skill.

> [!warning]
> A dull knife is more dangerous than a sharp one. A dull knife slips and requires extra pressure, increasing chance of losing control. A sharp knife cuts cleanly with minimal force.

## Grip and Posture

**The claw grip** — fingertips curl toward palm, knuckles forward. Your knuckles guide the blade; fingertips are safe. The rhythm: blade hits knuckle, knuckles push fingers back, advance grip, repeat. This takes 2 weeks to feel automatic; it feels unnatural at first.

**Knife hand** — rock or chop depending on motion. Rock (blade tip stays on board, handle moves up and down) for fine, controlled cuts. Chop (entire blade moves) for speed and power. Most vegetables ask for chop; some (herbs, fine garnish) ask for rock.

**Board position** — cut at a slight angle away from your body. Don't lean over; stand upright, bring work to neutral wrist. This prevents fatigue and carpal strain.

## Cuts and Their Purpose

**Julienne** — 2–3 mm square, 4–5 cm long. Visual refinement; quick cooking (thin = tender fast). Used for garnish, stir-fry, vegetable noodles.

**Brunoise** — 1–2 mm cube. Fine, delicate. Mirepoix (chopped aromatics) is a rough brunoise. Fine brunoise used for garnish or fine-diced vegetable bases.

**Bâtonnet** — 6 mm square, 5 cm long. Comfortable cut, visually clean. Potatoes for frites, carrots for blanching, standard vegetable cut.

**Batonnet-to-brunoise** — convert bâtonnet to brunoise by slicing across. Teaches progression.

**Chiffonade** — thin ribbon. Stack leafy herbs or lettuce, roll, slice across to make thin ribbons. Used for basil garnish, kale salads, finishing.

**Rough chop** — irregular, speed-based. Used for stock aromatics, anything that gets strained.

Each cut has a purpose: speed (chop), visual appeal (julienne), cooking uniformity (consistent size = consistent texture). Don't cut randomly; choose cuts intentionally.

## Sharpness and Slicing

A sharp knife slices cleanly through skin (tomato, citrus). A dull knife crushes, bruising flesh and squeezing juice. This matters more than you think: a crushed tomato weeps; a sliced tomato stays tight on the plate.

For scoops and wedges (citrus, avocado, tomato), sharpness matters. For rough cuts destined for stock or sauce, it's less critical.

## Left-Handed Work

Southpaws reverse all hand positions (claw hand on left, knife on right; board angled opposite). Mirror the technique, respect the difference. Don't force right-handed methods; it's slow and unsafe.

## Speed and Progression

Week 1–2: rough chop, sloppy cuts, learning muscle memory.
Week 3–4: consistent bâtonnet, deliberate rhythm, starting to relax.
Month 2: julienne, chiffonade, smooth flow. Speed comes from rhythm, not strength.

Rushing kills speed. A relaxed, deliberate pace builds muscle memory and reflex. Fast work comes later, from repetition.
