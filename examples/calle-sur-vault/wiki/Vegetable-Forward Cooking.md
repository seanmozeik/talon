# Vegetable-Forward Cooking

Vegetable-forward doesn't mean vegetarian. It means vegetables are the primary flavor and conceptual anchor, not the side. At Calle Sur, it's structural: we think about the vegetable first, then ask "what supports this?" instead of "what vegetable goes with this protein?"

This approach is cheaper (vegetables < proteins), more seasonal (vegetables have clear seasons; proteins can be sourced year-round), and, frankly, more interesting when done right.

## The Shift

Traditional plating: protein center, vegetable supporting cast. Vegetable-forward plating: vegetable center, protein accent (if present). Examples:

- Not: "seared scallop with asparagus"
- But: "spring asparagus with seared scallop and brown butter"

The vegetable is the subject. The protein is the garnish.

This isn't ideology; it's economics and flavor. A good vegetable in season needs less manipulation than a protein does. A fermented spring onion is already interesting; you add acid and fat, and you're done. A scallop needs careful cooking, finishing fat, and sauce. Start with the vegetable; the protein job is easier.

## Vegetable Selection

We work with [[Salt Marsh Farm]] (Maria). Vegetables in season:
- **Spring** — onions, radishes, early greens, peas, artichokes
- **Summer** — tomatoes, zucchini, eggplant, peppers, stone fruit
- **Fall** — roots, brassicas, mushrooms, apples, squash
- **Winter** — stored roots, hardy greens, citrus, preserved vegetables

See [[Seasonality Calendar]] for full rotation.

Each vegetable has a natural flavor profile (fermented onion is tangy and sharp; raw radish is peppery and crisp) and a preparation that honours it. Roasting concentrates and [[Maillard Reaction|browns]]. Fermenting (see [[Lacto-Fermentation]]) adds complexity and preserves. Raw emphasizes crispness and freshness.

## Preparation Methods

**Raw** — freshness, crispness, peppery or sweet depending on vegetable. Use when vegetable is at peak season and sweet (peas, radish, apple). Dress with acid and fat (see [[Salt Acid Fat Heat]]).

**Blanched** — tenderize, bright color retention, control doneness. Use for vegetables that are good raw but better with slight cook (green beans, asparagus, broccoli). Shock in ice water to stop cooking. Dress warm or cold.

**Roasted** — [[Maillard Reaction]] browning, caramelization, depth. Use for starch-heavy vegetables (root vegetables, winter squash, tomato). Toss with oil and salt, roast at 190–210°C until golden. Finish with acid (lemon, ferment juice, vinegar) and fresh herb.

**Fermented** — see [[Lacto-Fermentation]]. Complex, tangy, preserved. Use as condiment, side, or primary element (fermented onion can anchor a plate).

**Braised** — low, slow heat with stock or cooking liquid. Use for tough vegetables (root vegetables, brassicas, winter greens). The braising liquid becomes sauce.

## Examples from [[Spring 2026 Menu]]

**[[Dish - Charred Spring Onion]]** — fermented spring onion (char adds depth), whipped curd base (dairy support), fermented onion juice (acid and umami), herb oil (finish). The vegetable is the protein substitute; a little protein support makes the plate satisfying.

**[[Dish - Fava and Whey]]** — fava mousse (starch-heavy vegetable as base), poached egg (soft protein), whey foam (dairy element, acid), herb garnish. Spring-specific; favas are the story, egg is the nuance.

**Charred root salad (not in manifest but mentioned)** — roasted beets, carrots, radish (fermented, charred, raw in three states), dressed with ferment juice and oil. No protein. The vegetables in different states create interest and texture.

## Texture and Contrast

Vegetable-forward plates need textural variation:
- **Soft** — mousse, puree, braised element
- **Crisp** — raw, fried, roasted crust
- **Yielding** — braised but not mushy, roasted tender but firm

A plate of all soft vegetables feels mushy. A plate with soft base + crisp garnish + yielding center = interest.

> [!tip]
> The vegetable isn't more important because it's the star; it's important because it *is* the star. Treat it with the care you'd give a protein: season properly (see [[Salt Acid Fat Heat]]), cook it to intentional doneness (not "until soft"), finish it deliberately (acid, fat, garnish). The difference between "vegetable side" and "vegetable primary" is intention, not ingredients.

^vegetable-as-star

## Costing Advantage

A spring onion ferment costs roughly $2 for a week's batch (onions + salt). Plated as primary, it serves 10–15 covers. Cost per cover: $0.15. A 12 oz scallop costs $6, serves 1 cover. Protein cost alone is $6.

If the dish is "charred spring onion + scallop," the vegetable is cheap and interesting; the protein is supplement. Total plate cost: $6.15 (onion + scallop + garnish + sauce). If the dish is "spring onion as primary with scallop as accent," the math feels right; if it's "scallop with vegetable side," the vegetable feels obligatory and cheap.

Frame the menu correctly, and vegetable-forward is both cheaper and more interesting.
