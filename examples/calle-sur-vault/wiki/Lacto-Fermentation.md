# Lacto-Fermentation

Lacto-fermentation is the simplest controlled fermentation: vegetables + salt + time + submersion. Lactobacillus bacteria (present on all plants) become dominant under anaerobic conditions, producing lactic acid. Result: preserved, tangy, complex vegetables that last months.

The method is ancient, reliable, and scalable from a single jar to restaurant volumes. We run 8–10 ferments cycling through the walk-in.

## The Basic Method

1. **Prepare vegetables** — clean, trim, keep whole or cut into ferment-able pieces. Don't remove all surface microbes; you need them.
2. **Calculate salt** — 2–3% by weight of vegetables. For 1 kg vegetables, use 20–30 g salt.
3. **Combine** — massage salt into vegetables if cutting-heavy (cabbage, radish); layer whole vegetables (carrots, green onions) with salt.
4. **Pack a jar** — densely. Weight vegetables below brine line (wooden spoon, glass weight, or salt-packed cabbage leaf works). ^packing-weight
5. **Cover** — cloth or loose lid (allows gas escape, prevents dust). Seal tightly after first 3 days.
6. **Ferment** — 16–22°C is ideal. Warmer = faster = harsher. Colder = slower = subtler.
7. **Taste** — after day 5–7, taste daily. Harvest when the acid balance feels right.

## Why It Works

Vegetables are naturally coated with bacteria, especially Lactobacillus plantarum and L. mesenteroides. Salt triggers osmosis: water leaves the vegetable's cells, creating brine. Under anaerobic conditions (submerged in brine), lactobacilli thrive while molds and aerobic pathogens suffocate. They produce lactic acid as a metabolic waste, lowering pH to 3.5–4.0. Acidification is the kill-switch for botulism and most pathogens.

The fermentation is *self-regulated*—bacteria make conditions hostile for themselves once acid is high enough. This is why ferments plateau naturally.

## Variables That Matter

**Salt concentration** — below 2%, pathogens may colonize; above 3.5%, fermentation slows dramatically. We use 2.5% for most vegetables.

**Temperature** — directly controls fermentation speed. 
- 10–15°C: 4–8 weeks (slow, subtle, sharp acid)
- 16–22°C: 2–4 weeks (balanced)
- 23–28°C: 1–2 weeks (fast, punchy, risk of over-softening)

Room temperature (often 18–20°C) is our default. Cold walk-in slows ferments mid-cycle to preserve them.

**Submersion** — vegetables above brine line get fuzzy with kahm yeast or white mold. Skim off surface growth if light. If extensive or dark, toss the batch.

**Chlorinated water** — kills bacteria. We use filtered water. Tap water varies by location; some runs chlorine-heavy.

> [!warning]
> Never ferment in metal containers (aluminum, copper, mild steel). Salt + bacteria + acid = corrosion + metallic flavors. Use glass, ceramic, or food-grade plastic.

## Flavor Development Arc

**Days 1–3** — Lag phase. Subtle sour. Vegetables still crisp.

**Days 4–10** — Log phase. Acid ramps. Ferment becomes visibly cloudy (lactobacilli in suspension). This is healthy. Tanginess is aggressive. We often harvest here for the brightness.

**Weeks 2–4** — Plateau phase. Acid peaks and stabilizes. Secondary fermentation begins. Flavor deepens: umami notes emerge, complexity grows. Texture softens slightly.

**Month 2+** — Integration. Funky, settled, almost wine-like in subtle cases. Useful for sauces or as condiment.

Taste every few days. Fermentation doesn't have a finish line; it has *choices*. Same jar tastes different on day 5 vs. day 20.

## Troubleshooting

**Slime or rope texture** — acetobacter (vinegar bacteria) or mold. Sometimes recoverable: strain, taste, decide. If smell is off (not pleasantly sour), toss.

**Mushy vegetables** — over-fermented or contaminated. Temperature likely too high. Return the jar to 16°C and monitor.

**Mold blooming on surface** — kahm yeast (white, fuzzy). Harmless; skim and re-submerge. If dark or fuzzy (aspergillus), toss the jar.

**No fermentation after 10 days** — chlorinated water, too cold (below 12°C), weak salt, or old vegetables with few bacteria. Restart with filtered water, warmer spot, 2.5% salt.

**Explosion/overflow** — gases building faster than expected, lid too tight. Switch to cloth cover for first week, then cap loosely.
