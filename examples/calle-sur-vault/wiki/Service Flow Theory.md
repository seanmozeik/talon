# Service Flow Theory

Service flow is rhythm. A 40-seat restaurant service (2 seatings, 6 PM and 8:30 PM) is not chaos; it's a choreography. Tickets arrive in clusters. Each ticket describes the order. The kitchen reads tickets, fires components in sequence, plates at the pass, and sends plates to the table in tempo with front-of-house (pacing, table management).

Flow breaks when:
- Tickets pile up (no clear ticket hierarchy; everything fires at once)
- Mise runs out (no backup component; fire stalls)
- Cooking times are unpredictable (varies by 3 mins; service backs up)
- Plating is inconsistent (some covers wait while others plate in 90 seconds)

Flow works when:
- Tickets are read clearly and fires are staged
- [[Mise en Place Philosophy|Mise]] is deep (backup components ready)
- Cooking times are consistent and communicated
- Plating is a sequence, not an improvisation

## Ticket Hierarchy and Staging

A server enters an order (Table 4, 2 covers, 1 protein, 1 vegetable). The order is printed on a ticket and sent to the kitchen. The kitchen reads it in context of what's already cooking.

**First course** — fires first (25–30 mins before entrée). Components are prepped during any slow window.

**Entrée** — fires at a calculated delay so everything is ready at roughly the same time (4–6 min fire window, longer overall timing).

**Dessert** — fires after entrée is plated and sent (5–10 min after entrée plate leaves the pass).

Timing overlap is deliberate: while the first course is plating, the entrée is cooking. While entrée is being eaten, dessert is prepped. This is flow: staggered cooking, synchronized plating and service.

## Mise as Buffer

[[Mise en Place Philosophy]] is where flow lives. If all sauce is pre-made, a cook doesn't spend 8 mins making sauce during service; they plate in 90 seconds. If garnish is prepped and dry, it doesn't wilt; it goes on the plate fresh. If components are pre-seasoned, plating is a sequence, not a decision.

Mise runs out, though. If you expect 40 covers and prep for 40, cover 34 breaks service. Prep for 48; use the excess in staff meal or next-day prep. This is not waste; it's service insurance.

## Communication and Timing Callouts

The sous (or lead cook) calls timing:

- "Two entrée in" — two tickets just fired; cook starts protein
- "One in the window" — one plate is about to go up (3 mins out)
- "Hands!" — everyone assembles components and readies to plate

This language is shared discipline. Every cook knows "in the window" means 3 mins; no surprises.

> [!tip]
> Timing is taught by repetition and by understanding the components. A seared scallop: 90 seconds on each side, rest 2 mins. A roasted vegetable (prepped): 2 mins in the pass warmer to heat, then plate. A sauce reduction: already done, just portioned. These times add up; knowing them makes timing calls accurate.

## Pass Dynamics

The pass is where plates collect before service. A clean pass is orderly (plated items organized by table, ready to go). A messy pass is stressful (plates everywhere, no clear order, server confusion).

The chef (or lead plater) owns the pass. Plates don't leave the pass until:
1. All components are present
2. All elements are at correct temperature
3. Plating is correct (composition, garnish, sauce)
4. Chef has tasted (or visually inspected) for obvious errors

A plate leaving the pass with cold sauce or missing garnish is a service failure. The pass is the last quality gate before a plate hits the table.

## Pacing and Front-of-House

The kitchen doesn't control pacing alone. Front-of-house (servers, GM, sommelier) controls table pacing. A table should finish a course before the next course is fired. If diners are slow (chatty, savoring), next course fires later. If diners are pacing quickly, next course fires earlier.

Communication between back and front is essential. A server calls the kitchen: "Table 7 is 5 mins away from finishing first course; fire entrée in 4 mins." The kitchen knows the window and times accordingly.

Without this communication, either food waits (gets cold, looks sad) or food arrives before a table is ready (bad pacing experience).

## Covers Per Hour and Scalability

Service capacity is limited by:
- **Mise prep time** — how many components can be made in advance
- **Cooking time** — slowest component determines cook-through time
- **Plating time** — can plating happen in parallel (multiple platers) or serial (one person)
- **Ticket volume** — how many tickets can be read and staged simultaneously

A vegetable-forward menu with pre-made sauce and quick-cooking protein can handle higher covers/hour than a protein-heavy menu with slow braises and complex plating.

Understanding these constraints lets you design a service model that works. A 40-seat restaurant with 90-min service windows can handle 80–100 covers/day. A 40-seat with 2-hour service windows handles 60–80. Design accordingly.

## Service Failures and Recovery

Service fails when:
- A component breaks (a sauce breaks [emulsion fails], a vegetable gets overcooked)
- Ticket is misread (wrong protein fired, wrong timing)
- Mise runs out (no backup garnish, sauce is gone)

Recovery:
- **Small fix** (minor garnish issue) — fix on the plate before it leaves the pass
- **Medium fix** (component remake) — remake the component (1–2 mins), hold other plates in a warm zone, then plate and send
- **Large fix** (rebuild plate) — abort current plate, remake from scratch (3–5 mins), communicate timing to server and table

The server (front-of-house) communicates delay to the table. Honesty is better than silent waiting. "We're remaking a component; expect another 3 minutes" is professional; silence is anxiety.
