# Koji and Aspergillus

Koji (麹) is rice, grain, or legume colonized by Aspergillus oryzae mold. The mold secretes powerful enzymes—amylase (breaks starch to sugar) and protease (breaks protein to amino acids, free glutamate). The result: pre-digested substrates that taste sweet, umami-rich, almost savory without fermentation.

Koji is the foundation of miso, amazake, sake, and mirin. It's also the cleanest way to add umami without fermented fish paste or monosodium glutamate.

## Why Aspergillus Oryzae

This mold is non-toxic and has been used for centuries in East Asian food culture. Other Aspergillus species are dangerous (A. flavus produces aflatoxins). A. oryzae is domesticated and safe.

The mold prefers:
- Cooked or soaked grain (moisture + starch)
- Warm temperature (26–32°C)
- High humidity
- Some air circulation

Under these conditions, mycelium colonizes the grain within 2–3 days, producing visible white fuzz and a sweet, chestnut-like aroma.

## Making Koji

**Ingredients:**
- Short-grain white rice (or barley, chickpeas, etc.)
- Koji spores (koji-kin powder, available from suppliers)

**Method:**

1. **Soak and steam** — soak rice 4–6 hours, steam until grains are cooked but still separate (25–30 mins in steamer). Cool to 30°C.
2. **Inoculate** — dust koji spores lightly over cooled rice (typically 1–2% by weight).
3. **Incubate** — cover with cloth, place in warm, humid spot (26–30°C). A dehydrator on low, a heating pad + towel, or a proofing box works.
4. **Monitor** — by day 2, white mycelium should be visible. By day 3–4, full colonization. Sweet chestnut smell = success.
5. **Dry or use fresh** — koji rice can be dried (lasts months) or used immediately (lasts 3–5 days refrigerated).

The koji is ready when mycelium is thick, rice grains are held together by white fuzz, and aroma is sweet (not vinegary or rotten).

> [!tip]
> Don't worry about contamination if the koji smells sweet and looks fluffy. A. oryzae is aggressive; it outcompetes most contaminants. If mold looks gray, green, or stinks, toss it.

## Koji as Ingredient

**Koji rice** — can be:
- Eaten fresh as a sweet paste (natural sweetness, no added sugar)
- Blended into amazake (sweet rice drink)
- Fermented with salt for miso (months-long fermentation)
- Mixed with koji-inoculated soybeans for miso (months)
- Soaked in water to create koji liquid (sweet, subtly floral)

**Shio koji (salt koji)** — koji rice + salt fermented for 1–2 weeks. Produces brine rich in free amino acids and sugars. Use as seasoning, marinade base, or sauce ingredient. A spoonful adds umami depth without fish.

**Amazake** — koji rice blended with warm water, strained. A sweet beverage with rice milk texture. Fermentable; sometimes inoculated with lactobacilli for tangy amazake.

## Enzyme Power

The enzymes in koji are industrially useful but also delicate. Boiling kills them. Cooking above 70°C denatures them. If you want enzymatic action (breaking down proteins in stock, softening legumes), use koji fresh or at low heat. If you want flavor (not enzymatic activity), dried koji is stable and cost-effective.

## Flavor Profile

Koji tastes fundamentally different from bacterial fermentation:
- **Sweet** — due to glucose and oligosaccharides from starch breakdown
- **Umami** — free glutamate and nucleotides (inosinate)
- **Complex** — chestnut, honey, toast, subtle funky notes
- **Clean** — no acetic acid bite; no barnyard funk unless co-fermented

When koji is *then* fermented (as in miso), the acids from lactobacilli integrate with koji's sweetness and umami, creating the deep savory profile of aged miso.

## Koji in the Restaurant

We've explored koji shio (salt koji) as a marinade and meat tenderizer. A few grams mixed into a lamb marinade breaks down collagen while adding subtle sweetness. It's less punchy than brine but more interesting than salt alone.

[[Dish - Lamb Neck]] tested koji shio in development; the texture was tender, flavor was clean (no fish funk), and diners couldn't identify the ingredient—it just tasted right.

> [!note]
> Koji isn't a substitute for fermentation; it's a complement. Koji adds sweetness and umami. Bacterial fermentation adds acid, complexity, and preservation. Many traditional dishes (miso, mirin, sake) use both.

## Safety Notes

- Buy spores from reputable suppliers (tempeh kits, koji suppliers, specialty fermentation sites)
- Don't wild-ferment koji unless you're certain about spore identification
- A. oryzae is safe; other Aspergillus species are not. When in doubt, discard
- Dried koji is shelf-stable for months; fresh koji lasts 3–5 days refrigerated
