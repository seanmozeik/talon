# Mise en Place Philosophy

Mise en place—"everything in its place"—is obsessive prep. Before a single order hits the pass, every ingredient is prepped, portioned, seasoned, and positioned exactly where hands reach it. Mise discipline separates kitchens that flow from kitchens that panic.

Mise isn't busywork. It's the reason a cook can fire ten covers without thinking. It's the difference between a chef and a line cook.

## The Practice

Every station has a mise list. For a vegetable-forward station (ours), it might read:

- Fermented spring onions (charred, cooled), 4 oz portions in small containers
- Fava puree (blended, strained, room temp), 3 oz portions
- [[Lacto-Fermentation]] brine (strained, bright), 1 oz in squeeze bottle
- Salt (fine sea salt), pinch in small bowl
- Finishing oil (green oil or house-made herb blend), 0.5 oz in small squeeze bottle
- Garnish (microgreens, edible flowers, herbs), prepped and dry, in hotel pan

Each item is prepped the *exact* way it'll be served—temperature, texture, portion size. Nothing is approximated during service. A cook doesn't taste and adjust salt on plating; salt is already dialed in at mise.

> [!tip]
> Mise isn't perfection; it's intention. If something tastes off under heat (the ferment juice lost brightness in the walk-in), remake it. Mise is the buffer between prep and service, not the excuse to serve yesterday's prep.

^mise-intention

## Stations and Ownership

Each cook owns a station: vegetable station, protein station, sauce station, plating. Each cook is responsible for their mise and for restocking mid-service (checking containers, remaking components if levels drop, tasting for freshness).

A cook who walks to the pass without their station's mise clean and full is creating liability. The chef (or sous, or senior cook) trusts that when they call a cover, every component is ready.

## Prep Timing and Temperature

Cold components (salads, chilled soups, room-temp vegetables) are prepped first, hours ahead, giving time for flavors to settle. Warm or finishing components (sauces, proteins, garnishes) are prepped last (30–60 mins before service) so they're fresh.

Temperature control is part of mise: a reduction should be warm (140–160°C) at plating, not cold. A fermented vegetable should be room temp or slightly warm. A sauce should coat a spoon (right viscosity). All of this is staged during prep, not improvised during service.

## Quantities and Covers

Mise is calculated for expected covers + 20%. If you expect 40 covers and prep for 40, you're out of components by cover 34. Prep for 48. Leftover mise is used in staff meal or prepped for the next day. Waste is in prep, not in service panic.

## Mise and Costing

A mise list quantifies ingredients per cover. A vegetable station for 40 covers might use:
- 2 lbs fermented spring onions (cost: $2 wholesale, $0.05/oz = $6.40)
- 1 lb fava puree (cost: $3 wholesale, $0.19/oz = $3.04)
- 8 oz fermentation brine (cost: $0.50/liter = $0.13)
- Herbs and garnish: $2
- Oil/finishing: $1.50

**Total per cover**: roughly $0.32 in mise components. At a 40-cover night, mise cost is $12.80. Document this; it's real cost, not fantasy.

## Walk-In Organization

Mise containers are labeled (component name, prep date, use-by date), organized by station in the same walk-in zone. Each station area has a clip board with mise count (what's needed) and a tally (what's prepped). Before service, a quick visual sweep confirms: all containers present, all labeled, all at right temperature.

This sounds obsessive because it is. It's also the difference between a 6:15 PM service start (everything ready) and a 6:35 PM start (playing catch-up).

## Scaling and Failure

A single dinner for 8 people at home is roughly 1 cover per person. A restaurant dinner for 40 covers is 5× the mise complexity, 5× the logistics. Mise discipline scales from family meal to 80-cover nights because the philosophy is the same: intentional, prepped, positioned, ready.

When service breaks (a supplier misses a delivery), mise discipline helps you adapt: you know exactly what you have, when it runs out, and what substitutions are possible. Without mise, a disruption becomes chaos.

## Mise and [[Knife Skills Canon]]

Knife prep *is* mise. Consistent cuts mean consistent cooking, which means consistent plating. A brunoise carrot cooks at a predictable rate; a rough chop is a variable. Knife work is where mise begins.
