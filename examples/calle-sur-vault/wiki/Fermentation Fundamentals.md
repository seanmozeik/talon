# Fermentation Fundamentals

Fermentation is controlled microbial transformation of food substrates—the deliberate cultivation of bacteria, yeasts, and molds to preserve, transform flavor, and create complexity. It's not magic; it's microbiology + salt + time.

We run fermentation programs at Calle Sur because they solve two problems at once: preservation and flavor development. A fermented carrot isn't a preserved carrot masquerading as flavor—it's a different vegetable entirely.

## Why Fermentation Works

Fermentation creates an environment hostile to pathogens while favoring beneficial microorganisms. The key conditions:

- **Anaerobic (oxygen-free) environment** — prevents mold and aerobic spoilage
- **High salt concentration** — osmotic pressure selects for salt-tolerant microbes (like lactobacilli)
- **Acidification** — lactic acid produced by bacteria drops pH below 4.6, stopping pathogenic growth
- **Temperature stability** — cold ferments develop slowly and deliberately; warm ferments accelerate

When these conditions align, you get a stable, flavored substrate that lasts weeks or months. The metabolic byproducts of these microbes—lactic acid, acetic acid, complex alcohols—are why the ferment tastes alive. ^fermentation-conditions

## Lactobacillus: The Workhorse

[[Lacto-Fermentation]] is the most common method in the kitchen because lactobacilli are everywhere (on vegetables, in salt, in the air) and they're aggressive colonizers. They produce lactic acid aggressively, creating that bright, tangy note. They're also salt-tolerant and speed up at temps between 16-22°C.

The name "lacto-fermentation" is misleading—it has nothing to do with dairy. It's named for lactic acid, the byproduct.

## Koji and Mold Fermentation

[[Koji and Aspergillus]] represents a different frontier: using mold to pre-digest starches and proteins into sugar and amino acids. Koji rice, miso, amazake—these leverage enzymatic power before (or instead of) bacteria. Slower, more specialized, but produces umami and sweetness that bacteria alone can't.

## Flavor Development

Fermentation doesn't just preserve—it *changes*. The flavor arc matters:

1. **Days 1–3** — Lag phase. Microbes adjust. Subtly sour.
2. **Days 4–14** — Log phase. Rapid acid production. Tanginess peaks.
3. **Week 3+** — Plateau. Secondary fermentation. Complexity emerges: funky notes, barnyard hints, mouth-feel from glycerol.

You taste these stages; each one is useful. We harvest some ferments young (bright, aggressive acid) and others at week 6 (settled, integrated).

> [!tip]
> Don't obsess over fermentation as "natural" or "alive." Call it what it is: controlled microbiology. Respect the microbiology, and it respects you.

## Common Kitchen Ferments

- **Vegetables** — [[Lacto-Fermentation]] (carrots, cabbage, green tomatoes, spring onions)
- **Grains** — [[Koji and Aspergillus]] (miso, koji rice), [[Bread and Doughs]] (sourdough levain)
- **Hot sauce** — [[Fermented Hot Sauce Line]] blends bacteria + time + heat
- **Stocks and sauces** — Aged [[Sauce Mothers]] develop via slow bacterial action
- **Fish and meat** — Garum, miso paste, salted ferments (advanced, specialty equipment needed)

## Troubleshooting

**White mold on surface** — kahm yeast or benign surface mold. Skim it. Common when brine weak or veg exposed to air.

**Soft vegetables** — over-fermented or contaminated. Toss it. Temperature too high accelerates softening.

**No sourness after 2 weeks** — cold ferment, weak salt (try 3% by weight), or water too chlorinated. Restart with filtered water and check temperature.

**Ropiness (slimy texture, acetic acid smell)** — some acetobacter contamination. Usually still safe; taste first. These bacteria are acidifying too, just differently.

## Salt's Role

[[Salt Acid Fat Heat]] covers this properly, but in fermentation salt does three things: osmosis (draws liquid out), selectivity (favors lactobacilli), and integration (bonds with other molecules, smooths flavor). Standard ferment ratio is 2–3% salt by weight of vegetables. Don't skimp or over-salt—both fail.

## Storage and Shelf Life

A well-fermented, properly sealed jar lasts:
- Room temperature: 2–3 months (taste and check)
- Refrigerated: 6–12 months (slow acids prevent spoilage)
- Frozen: indefinitely (texture suffers slightly; flavor intact)

Lactobacilli are lazy in the cold; they slow to near-dormancy, which halts flavor evolution. Use this to your advantage: ferment warm, rest cold.
