# Noma Fermentation Lab: Inside the Kitchen's Koji Workshop

**The New Yorker** | February 2026

In a corner of the Noma test kitchen in Copenhagen—a room most diners never see—the walls are lined with glass jars in various stages of transformation. Some contain koji mold growing white and fragrant across beds of rice. Others hold the amber-tinted garum that took four months to develop. There are bottles of miso in phases of aging, and test batches of what René Redzepi half-jokingly calls "experiments that taste like the earth's fever dreams."

At the helm is **Lars Williams**, head of fermentation research at Noma, a man who talks about umami compounds the way a sommelier talks about vintage years.

"We started asking ourselves: what if fermentation isn't a preservation technique? What if it's a flavor architecture?" Williams says, gesturing to a shelf where koji colonies glow faintly white under grow lights.

## Koji: The Foundation

Koji—the mold *Aspergillus oryzae*—is the foundation of Japanese fermentation. It produces powerful enzymes that break down proteins and starches into amino acids and sugars. At Noma, they've been experimenting with koji on unconventional substrates: not just rice, but barley, chestnuts, even dried mushrooms.

"The substrate changes everything," Williams explains. "Rice koji is gentle and floral. Barley koji is mineral and green. We grew koji on dried porcini, and it took on this earthy, almost truffle-like intensity."

The Noma team cultivates koji in open air, in wooden trays, using a humidity-controlled chamber. The process takes five to seven days. When ready, the koji smells sweet and faintly buttery.

## Garum: Umami Alchemy

Perhaps the most ambitious project in the Noma fermentation lab is their garum—a fish sauce descended from the Roman condiment. Using local Nordic fish scraps, salt, and time, they've created a sauce that Redzepi describes as "aggressive, beautiful, and unforgettable."

The method is simple in theory, relentless in execution. Layer fish, salt, koji (as enzyme), and let sit for months in a cool, dark corner. The koji and salt work in concert: salt draws moisture, koji breaks down protein. The result is a liquor of astonishing depth.

"We're not trying to replicate Rome," Williams says. "We're trying to understand fermentation at a molecular level, then apply that understanding to our region, our ingredients."

## The Experiments Continue

Other jars contain experiments that won't reach the menu for years, if ever. Redzepi and Williams are exploring miso made from seed butters, lacto-fermented herbs, even koji-koji (koji growing on koji). Some will fail. Some will surprise.

"The magic," Williams says, "is that fermentation is conversation between human intention and microbial life. You create the conditions; the mold and bacteria write the rest."
