# The Salt Industry Nobody Talks About

**The Atlantic** | March 2026

Salt is one of those commodities we take for granted until we can't get it. For centuries, coastal regions produced their own sea salt through solar evaporation—a quiet, patient process that required knowledge passed down through families. But in the past three decades, industrial salt production has consolidated around a handful of massive operations, and artisanal producers have nearly vanished from the landscape.

## The Consolidation

In the U.S., roughly 70 percent of salt is now produced by three mega-corporations that mine rock salt from underground deposits or extract it through chemical-intensive solar operations. These operations prioritize volume and consistency over flavor or ecological sensitivity. A ton of salt is a ton of salt—interchangeable, standardized, flavorless.

"We've industrialized something that shouldn't be industrialized," says Maria Gutiérrez, who runs a small solar salt operation in Baja California Sur. "Sea salt isn't just sodium chloride. It's minerals, it's place, it's the ecosystem of the estuary you're pulling from."

Gutiérrez's salt business employed fifteen people thirty years ago. Today it's her and a part-time assistant. "Young people don't want to do this work. It's not lucrative. And the corporations have made it nearly impossible to compete by flooding the market with cheap industrial salt."

## What We Lose

The minerals in artisanal sea salt—calcium, magnesium, potassium—aren't just flavor. They're nutrition. Industrial salt is often washed repeatedly and treated with anti-caking agents; all the mineral complexity is stripped away.

For chefs, the differences are audible. "Sea salt has a sound," says Dominique Blanc, a Michelin-starred chef in Provence. "Industrial salt is flat. Artisanal salt has a mineral quality, almost a resonance with the food. It sounds strange, but your palate knows."

## The Resistance

A small but growing movement of chefs, artisanal producers, and food activists is pushing back. Some have begun establishing direct relationships with remaining small-scale salt makers. Others are reviving old methods—solar evaporation in controlled ponds—as a response to industrial salt's homogeneity.

In Ria Formosa, Portugal, a cooperative of salt makers has organized a collective marketing push to reach restaurants in Europe and the U.S. Their salt carries a traceability certificate: you know where it came from, who made it, which tide it was harvested from.

"We're not asking consumers to pay premium prices for salt because it's rare," says João Silva, the cooperative's director. "We're asking them to understand that salt—like wine, like cheese—has terroir. Your dish is only as good as your salt."
