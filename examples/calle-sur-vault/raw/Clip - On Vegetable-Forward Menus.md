# On Vegetable-Forward Menus: Why Vegetables Are the Future of Restaurant Food

**By Yotam Ottolenghi** | April 2026

There was a time when a restaurant menu centered on vegetables was an apology. A vegetarian accommodation, something for guests who didn't eat meat. Now, a growing number of the world's most accomplished cooks are designing menus where vegetables are not supporting actors but protagonists—and the food is more exciting for it.

## The Shift

The shift isn't purely ethical, though ethics matter. It's culinary. Vegetables, when you stop thinking of them as a course to fill space and start thinking of them as a primary ingredient, demand a different skillset from a kitchen. They require precision: knowing when to cook and when to serve raw. They require restraint: a good vegetable dish should amplify the vegetable, not bury it. And they require *engagement* with season and supply in a way that meat-driven cooking doesn't demand.

When your menu is built around spring peas, summer tomatoes, autumn squash, and winter roots, you are architecting a menu that changes. Your suppliers become collaborators. Your farmers are your creative partners.

## The Technique Gap

Here's what surprised me, working with vegetable-driven kitchens: most cooks are still thinking like carnivores. They haven't developed the grammar of vegetable cookery. A beef cheek is forgiving; you can braise it hard and it becomes tender. Braise a carrot the same way and it becomes sludge. A whole fish can yield days of cooking across a menu; a single vegetable needs to be handled with more nuance and variety.

This is a skill gap, not a talent gap. Vegetable cooking is harder because it requires more precision. But that difficulty is also where the beauty lives.

## The Economics

Vegetable-forward menus also shift the economics of restaurants in interesting ways. Vegetables are cheaper to source than meat, but also more variable. You can't build a menu on frozen peas in April; you have to work with what's in season. This makes supply chain relationships essential. It makes forecasting harder. But it also means that vegetable restaurants tend to have deeper, more reciprocal relationships with their suppliers.

The margin is different, too. A vegetable-driven menu won't have the profit margins of a steakhouse. But it allows for tighter relationships with suppliers, more flexibility in pricing, and less waste.

## The Philosophy

What I love about vegetable-forward menus isn't just the food. It's the philosophy underneath. It says: we are not trying to dominate our ingredients. We are trying to understand them. We are trying to let the vegetable speak, and then help it speak louder.

A charred spring onion with anchovy emulsion is not a "vegetable dish." It's a conversation between the sweet char of the onion, the umami of the anchovy, the salt, the acid. Each element is doing essential work.

This is the future of restaurant food. Not all restaurants will go vegetable-forward. But the ones that understand vegetables—that treat them as central rather than peripheral—those kitchens are going to define the next decade of cooking.
