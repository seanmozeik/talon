# NYT Lacto-Fermented Carrot Ribbons

**By Tejal Rao** | March 2026

The quiet magic of lacto-fermentation lives in patience. Not the patience of waiting for bread dough to proof, but the stranger, more subtle patience of letting salt and time do the work your hands cannot. These carrot ribbons, fermented for anywhere from five days to three weeks, become both more themselves and something entirely new.

## The Recipe

### Ingredients
- 2 lbs young, sweet carrots (rainbow if you can find them, but orange will do)
- 2 tbsp sea salt
- 1 tbsp coriander seeds, lightly crushed
- 3 or 4 dried chiles (árbol or similar)
- 6 cloves garlic, sliced thin
- A 1-inch piece of ginger, cut into matchsticks
- Filtered water
- Optional: 1 tsp yellow mustard seeds

### Method

1. **Prep the carrots.** Using a mandoline or vegetable peeler, shave carrots into long, thin ribbons. Don't peel them—the skin holds sweetness and texture. Place in a clean quart jar.

2. **Build the brine.** Dissolve 2 tbsp salt in 2 cups filtered water. The brine should taste pleasantly salty, like a light soup. This is your fermentation environment; get it right.

3. **Layer and salt.** Add coriander seeds, chiles, garlic, and ginger to the jar, scattering them among the carrots. Pour the brine over until vegetables are submerged. Weight the carrots down with a smaller jar filled with water, or use a fermentation weight. The key is keeping everything below the surface.

4. **Ferment.** Cover loosely with cheesecloth or a breathable cloth (not a lid). Leave at room temperature, away from direct sun. By day 3 you'll see bubbling. Taste at day 5. By day 7 they'll be tangy and crisp. By week 2–3, they deepen.

5. **Store.** Once fermented to your taste, cap and refrigerate. They'll keep for months.

## Notes from the Test Kitchen

What we loved: the brightness of these ribbons against rich proteins. The fermented carrot liquor—that brine—becomes an asset: use it in dressings, pickle other vegetables, or splash into soups.

The surprise was in the texture. Because you're not cooking them, they remain snappy, but the fermentation softens their vegetal aggressiveness in a way that raw never quite achieves.
