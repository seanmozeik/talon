# Voice Memo: Foraging Walk with Maria — April 12, 2026

**Recorded:** 2026-04-12, ~3 PM  
**Location:** Coastal cliffs, Salt Marsh Farm territory  
**Duration:** ~15 min (transcribed)

---

Okay, so we're here. The sun's out, wind's picking up off the water. Maria's pointing to the wild fennel—it's everywhere right now, tall, yellow flowers starting. She says this is the window. Peak pollen, maybe another three weeks before it gets too seedy.

Wild fennel pollen behind the dunes—Maria says save for the lamb dish. Says it's better than the cultivated stuff we buy. More anise-forward, sweeter. She's teaching me how to tell the flowering stage. Yellow flowers = good. Brown/seed stage = too late. Get it.

She's clipping some, putting it in a paper bag. The smell—just crushing it in my hands. Wow. Like fennel, but brighter. Less heavy than what we use now.

Moving down toward the rocks. She's looking for coastal mushrooms. Says the spring damp, the salt spray, it pulls certain fungi. She spots oyster mushrooms on a downed oak—pale, almost translucent. Says they're better here than anywhere inland. "The salt gets in them," she says. "Flavor's different."

Also showing me sea fennel—Crithmum maritimum—growing in the cracks. Edible, salty, peppery. Says chefs are using it now, but it's been food here forever. Nobody realized.

She's picking some. Wants me to taste it raw. Sharp. Mineral. Salty without added salt.

Back up to the cliff path. She's talking about the sustainability of this. The wild fennel will always be here. The mushrooms, too—you can't really deplete coastal fungi. But the way most foragers work, they're taking everything, not rotating, not leaving enough to seed. "This isn't a farm," she says. "But it's not infinite either."

She's suggesting we come back in May with Jonas. Maybe make a semi-regular thing. Spring and fall harvest walks, focusing on specific plants. Better than me sending her a shopping list. More in the bones.

Yeah. Do this.

She's also mentioning—the young garlic will be up in another week or so. We should plan around that too. She wants to know if we're doing anything special with it for the menu.

I'm saying, yeah. Something bright. Maybe something fermented. She's nodding. Says the young garlic ferments fast. Gentler than cured. Less sulfur.

Okay, heading back. It's a good walk.
