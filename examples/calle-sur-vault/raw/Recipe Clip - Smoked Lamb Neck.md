# Smoked Lamb Neck with Bay & Thyme Brine

**By Diana Henry** | Adapted from *Cook Simply*

---

## Brine

- 2 tbsp salt (use good salt)
- 1 tbsp brown sugar
- 3 bay leaves
- 6 sprigs fresh thyme
- 1 tsp black peppercorns, crushed
- 4 cloves garlic, sliced
- 1 tsp coriander seeds
- 1 tsp juniper berries
- 6 cups water

---

## For the Lamb

- 1 lamb neck (2.5–3 lbs), trimmed of excess fat
- 2 tbsp olive oil
- Sea salt and pepper
- Wood for smoking: apple or oak

---

## Method

### The Brine (Day 1)

1. Heat 2 cups of water. Dissolve salt and brown sugar. Cool completely.
2. Add remaining cold water, bay leaves, thyme, peppercorns, garlic, coriander, juniper.
3. Submerge lamb neck. Brine for 12–24 hours in the fridge.

### The Smoking (Day 2)

1. **Prepare the grill.** Set up for smoking: one side hot for searing, one side cool with indirect heat. Have wood chips soaking (30 min minimum).

2. **Remove from brine.** Pat lamb dry. Season lightly with salt and pepper (the brine is already salty).

3. **Sear the meat.** Heat the hot side of the grill until very hot. Sear lamb neck on all sides until browned, about 2 minutes per side. This builds flavor and a bark.

4. **Cold side smoking.** Move lamb to cool side. Add soaked wood chips to the hot side (place on coals or in a smoking box). Adjust vents: aim for 225–250°F.

5. **Smoke low and slow.** Smoke for 2.5–3 hours until the internal temperature reaches 165°F and the meat yields to a probe. The lamb should be tender, the exterior darkened.

6. **Rest.** Remove from grill and rest for 10–15 minutes before serving.

---

## Serving

Diana suggests serving with charred spring vegetables, a sharp grain mustard, and a spoonful of its own braising liquid if you've collected drippings.

---

## Marco's Notes

> [!note]
> **Brine is essential.** The salt+sugar+herbs soften the muscle fibers. Without it, neck is tough. With it, it becomes unctuous. Don't skip.

> [!note]
> **3 hours is long.** Test for tenderness at 2.5 hours. Thick-cut neck can vary. Want it just past yielding, not falling apart.

> [!note]
> **Use this brine philosophy for other things.** Bay + thyme + juniper works for pork, chicken, even vegetables. The combination tastes like spring.

> [!note]
> **Consider the wood.** Apple is gentle and sweet. Oak is more assertive. Try a mix, or use what we have. The brine's herbs will dominate the smoke profile anyway.
