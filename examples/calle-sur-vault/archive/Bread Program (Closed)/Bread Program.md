# Bread Program — Postmortem (Closed November 2025)

**Launched:** May 2025  
**Closed:** November 20, 2025  
**Duration:** 6.5 months  
**Status:** Abandoned

---

## The Vision

Marco wanted Calle Sur to bake its own bread. Not just baguettes for accompaniment, but a full line: sourdough starter maintained in-house, slow fermented doughs, a rotating selection of shapes and grains. Bread as part of the menu philosophy, not an afterthought.

He brought in Jonas from the line to run it, carving out a bread station and a small proof box. Lúcia was skeptical from the start.

---

## What Happened

### Phase 1: Enthusiasm (May–July 2025)

Jonas took to it. He built a starter from scratch (100% hydration, whole wheat base). By June, the breads were good—real structure, good color, consistent bake. Marco was excited. We offered:

- Sourdough boule (served family-style, sliced)
- Rye-spelt blend (nuttier, denser)
- Olive focaccia (high-hydration, lazy method)

Guests loved them. "Best bread we've had in months," was common feedback. The tab-add for bread was €4/person, and 85% of tables bought it.

Revenue: small but real. ~€80/service at 40 covers.

### Phase 2: Reality (August–September 2025)

The cracks appeared in late July. Bread production demanded:

- **Early start.** Dough mixed at 5 AM (Jonas arriving at 4:30 AM)
- **Two bulk ferments.** 4 hours cold + 2 hours room-temp, staggered around line prep
- **Three bake windows.** First batch at 10 AM (for lunch if applicable), then two more at 5 PM and 6:15 PM to stagger service bread
- **Cleanup.** 30 minutes post-service

This was 2–2.5 hours/day of Jonas's time, and he was already on the line. By August, he was coming in earlier, staying later. By early September, he was visibly tired.

Simultaneously, the sourdough starter had a bad week in late August (contamination scare, temperature spike). Jonas salvaged it, but the episode cost two days of full production. Marco had to buy emergency bread from an external supplier. The brand damage was internal but real—the whole point was consistency.

Lúcia pushed back. "This is insane. Jonas is exhausted. We're paying him €8/hour extra to start at 4:30 AM for bread that we might not even sell. And if the starter dies, what then?"

### Phase 3: Decay (October–November 2025)

By October, the honeymoon was over. Revenue held (~€75–80/service), but the costs were climbing:

- **Labor:** Jonas at 10 extra hours/week = €80/week
- **Ingredients:** Flour quality (+€15/week premium), hydration/waste margin
- **Equipment:** Proof box electricity, maintenance

Break-even was roughly €200/week in revenue, which we were hitting. But the margin was thin, and the single-dependency risk was huge: if Jonas got sick, or decided to leave, the whole program collapsed.

Marco tried to cross-train Lúcia in September. She did it reluctantly, made one batch, and said no. "I have enough on my plate."

By late October, Marco was baking bread himself on Sundays, trying to build a backup schedule. It didn't work—he had no time.

### Phase 4: Closure (November 2025)

Marco made the call on November 15, 2025: "We're closing the bread program. We can't sustain it."

Last bread service: November 20, 2025.

---

## The Brutal Math

**Revenue (6.5 months):**
- May–July: ~€1,920 (3 services/week, 12 weeks)
- August–November: ~€4,160 (4 services/week, 16 weeks)
- **Total:** ~€6,080

**Direct Costs:**
- Labor (Jonas premium): ~€2,080
- Ingredients & waste: ~€960
- Equipment (depreciated): ~€400
- **Total:** ~€3,440

**Gross profit:** ~€2,640 (43% margin)

**But:** The mental load on Marco + the strain on Jonas + the operational risk. None of that is captured in the math.

---

## Why It Failed

1. **Single baker dependency.** Bread knowledge was entirely Jonas's. No redundancy. No succession plan. In a 40-seat restaurant with two service days/week, that's reckless.

2. **Time poverty.** The kitchen was already tight. Carving out 2.5 hours/day for bread starved other stations. Prep quality suffered elsewhere.

3. **Starter risk.** A living culture requires attention. One bad week tanks the entire offering. We didn't have systems to handle that failure.

4. **Menu misalignment.** In hindsight, Marco realized that the bread program, while beautiful in isolation, didn't amplify the restaurant's vegetable-forward philosophy. It was a separate thing—beautiful, but separate.

5. **Stubbornness.** Marco held on longer than he should have. He loved the concept so much that he didn't pull the plug when Lúcia first objected in August. Two extra months of strain could have been avoided.

---

## What We Learned

1. **Passion ≠ Sustainability.** A beautiful idea doesn't survive if the operational infrastructure isn't there.

2. **Cross-training is non-negotiable.** If a program depends on one person, it will fail. Period.

3. **Time is scarcer than money.** The €2,640 gross profit looked good. But the 2.5 hours/day of kitchen time was worth more.

4. **Starter cultures are living things.** They need infrastructure: temperature control, feeding schedules, backup systems. Casual maintenance is insufficient.

5. **Know when to stop.** Marco's stubbornness cost him two months of strain. The decision in September should have been final.

---

## Epilogue

Jonas stayed on the line. He's relieved (though he'd never have said it). The kitchen rhythm returned to something sustainable. We source bread from a local bakery now (€0.75/unit cost, wholesale). Guests still get good bread. The tab-add is now €3, slightly lower, but the operational burden is gone.

Sometimes killing a beautiful idea is the right decision.

---

*Bread program closed November 20, 2025. No plans to revive.*
