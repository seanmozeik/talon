# Cassoulet with House-Made Confit Duck — Retrospective

**Period on Menu:** December 2025 – March 2026  
**Final Status:** Archived (removed March 15, 2026)

---

## The Dish

A stripped-down cassoulet: slow-braised cannellini beans, house-made duck confit, a loose-textured duck sausage made with Lúcia's spice blend, and a sharp mustard-breadcrumb crust. Served in a warm earthenware crock.

**Plate size:** Large (600g)  
**Selling price:** €42  
**Food cost:** ~€16 (duck legs €8, beans €1.50, sausage €4, breadcrumbs €0.50, stock €2)  
**Margin:** 62% (healthy)

---

## Why It Worked (December–January)

The dish opened with momentum. Cold weather, winter appetite, the comfort of a slow-cooked meal. Ordering ran 40–45% of mains in December. The crispness of the crust, the richness of the confit, the grain of the beans—it was cohesive.

Guests gave good feedback. It felt serious. Winter-appropriate. Several regulars requested it by name.

---

## Why It Faded (February–March)

Three problems converged:

### 1. Labor Intensity
- Duck confit required 2–3 hours active time + 3-day rest before service
- Sausage-making fell on Lúcia (already managing plating, line station)
- By February, we were prepping two cassoulets on Monday, two on Thursday
- Each prep added ~4 hours to the kitchen's week

### 2. Seasonal Appetite Shift
February brought milder weather. Fish orders climbed. The heavy cassoulet felt less needed. Orders dropped from 40% to 20% by March.

### 3. Supply Friction
Good duck legs became inconsistent after mid-February (supplier inventory thinned). We had to source emergency backup. Cost began creeping up (€8 → €9.50). Margin pressure started.

---

## The Decision

Marco rotated it off March 1. The math was simple: at 20% ordering ratio, the dish was using 6+ hours/week of kitchen labor for ~8 orders/service. That's inefficient.

"A good dish at the wrong moment is a bad dish," Marco said.

---

## What We Learned

1. **Luxury proteins in small kitchens need demand.** The cassoulet was technically perfect. But in a 40-seat room, serving 8 cassoulets/service doesn't justify the prep overhead.

2. **Seasonal appetite is real.** Winter cassoulet made sense. Spring cassoulet felt anachronistic.

3. **Fermentation beats confit (for us).** By contrast, the braised lamb neck with fermented garlic stayed on the menu throughout. It was less labor-intensive, more forgiving, and the fermentation angle felt more aligned with Marco's philosophy.

4. **Rotation keeps food fresh.** The dish didn't fail; it just had its season.

---

## Template for Revival

If cassoulet returns:
- Reduce portion (400g instead of 600g)
- Use commercial duck confit instead of house-made (if margin allows)
- Or, shift to a faster-cooking bean braise with sausage (remove the confit complexity)
