# Winter 2025 Menu — Retrospective

**Period:** December 2025 – March 2026  
**Status:** Archived (closed March 15, 2026)

---

## The Menu Lineup

The Winter 2025 menu was built around root vegetables, braised meats, and fermented condiments. We opened with a limited list of five dishes, expanded to seven by January, and eventually trimmed back to five by February as certain plates underperformed.

### Starters
- **Charred Beet with Whipped Goat Cheese & Black Walnut** — consistently strong
- **Cured Mushroom Consommé with Fried Shallot** — seasonal favorite; expensive to execute

### Mains
- **Cassoulet with House-Made Confit Duck** — strong early (Dec–Jan), faded Feb–Mar
- **Braised Lamb Neck with Wild Herbs & Fermented Garlic** — steady; never failed
- **Roasted Halibut with Celery Root Purée & Crispy Skin** — popular with fish guests, tight margin
- **Root Vegetable Tart with Comte & Thyme** (vegetarian option) — underperformed; only 2–3 orders/week

### Sides
- **Polenta with Bone Marrow & Pickled Onion** — added January; became default
- **Braised Endive with Anchovy Butter** — rotated off by late February (supply issue)

---

## What Sold

**Winners:**
- Beet starter (65–75% order rate when offered)
- Lamb neck main (consistent 30–40% ordering ratio)
- Polenta side (added to 90%+ of mains by February)

**Steady middle:**
- Halibut main (20–25% orders; fish traffic varies)
- Cassoulet (declining: 40% in Dec, 20% by March)

**Underperformers:**
- Mushroom consommé (expensive at €6 cost, priced at €22; only 1–2 orders/service by Feb)
- Vegetarian tart (never more than 3 orders/week; required advance notice for dietary guests)

---

## Labor & Cost Insights

### Cassoulet Postmortem
Early success masked unsustainable labor. The dish required:
- Pre-cooked duck confit (2–3 hours prep, then three-day rest before service)
- House-made sausage (made by Lúcia every Tuesday)
- Beans (overnight soak, 3-hour braise)

By February, Lúcia's time was stretched. We were prepping two days of cassoulets on Monday, two on Thursday. By mid-March, Marco made the call: rotate off. The dish sold at 20% by then anyway.

**Lesson:** Luxury proteins + multi-component preparation = risky for small kitchens unless you have dedicated labor or demand justifies it.

### Polenta Win
Low cost of goods (corn, stock, butter, bone marrow fat). ~€2 cost, €6 upcharge on plates. High-margin, quick to finish in service. By February, it became the kitchen's workhorse side.

---

## Financial Summary

**Period Revenue:** ~€87,000 (40 covers/night average, ~90 nights open)  
**Food Cost (blended):** 33% (target was 32%)  
**Labor (kitchen):** On target (Lúcia scaled staff over the season)

**Closing P&L (March 15, 2026):**
- Menu was running at break-even on food cost + labor
- Cassoulet removal freed ~6 hours/week of prep time
- Vegetarian tart removal reduced waste (we were throwing away ~30% of prepped vegetables)

---

## What We Learned

1. **Seasonal protein demands shift.** Fish orders increased as we moved into spring (even in late February). Duck felt right in December, tired by March.

2. **Luxury components need luxury demand.** The mushroom consommé was technically perfect, but cost + price point didn't align with diner expectations in a 40-seat room.

3. **Fermentation + braise is a winning combination.** The lamb neck with fermented garlic never wavered. It's a template worth repeating.

4. **Polenta is utility + elegance.** Simple, affordable, versatile. It became a silent hero of the menu.

5. **Rotate, don't hold on.** Cassoulet was good; it just didn't deserve February. Better to pivot than watch a good dish fade.

---

## Into Spring 2026

The Winter 2025 menu taught us to trust supply, cost our dishes tight, and rotate ruthlessly. Spring 2026 will feature more vegetables, lighter broths, and fewer components per plate. We're prepared.
